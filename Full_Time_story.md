# Full Time
### A Short Story

The stadium in Querétaro held sixty thousand people and one very specific problem, and the problem was sitting three rows back from the pitch wearing a referee's jersey that didn't fit him.

Cole didn't know the man's name yet. He knew the man had a Pikachu riding in his jacket pocket, sparking faintly every time the crowd noise spiked, and he knew the man's eyes kept drifting to the VIP boxes instead of the match. That was wrong. Everyone in this stadium had paid attention to one thing for ninety minutes. This guy had paid attention to twelve different things, and none of them were soccer.

Cole had been in town two days. He wasn't a fan, particularly. He'd come because a girl named Mara had sent him a message that said *something's wrong here, I can feel it*, and then had stopped answering. Mara traveled with a Sprigatito that rode on her shoulder like a scarf, and the two of them together had a way of noticing things before anyone else did. If Mara had felt something wrong three days ago, it was wrong now, multiplied.

He found her in the lower concourse, crouched by a maintenance door, Sprigatito's ears flat against its skull.

"There's a relay team," Mara said, without preamble. "Three of them. They're not here for the match."

"What are they here for?"

"The trophy ceremony. After the final whistle. There's a corridor that runs under the pitch — service access, normally locked — and somebody's rigged the door."

Cole looked at the door. It looked like a door. That was the thing about doors that had been rigged by people who knew what they were doing — they looked exactly like doors.

"Pikachu guy upstairs," Cole said. "Section 214. He's got a partner that can short out the stadium lighting rig if someone tells it to."

Mara's Sprigatito made a low sound, somewhere between a growl and a question.

"That's the plan," Cole said. "Lights go out for ninety seconds during the ceremony. Long enough to swap the actual trophy for whatever's in that crate I saw being wheeled in marked 'audio equipment.' Nobody notices in the dark. Everybody notices when the real trophy turns up missing two days later in a private auction in three different cities at once, because there isn't just one fake — there's three, going to three different buyers who all think they bought the real thing."

"That's insane," Mara said.

"That's a very profitable kind of insane," Cole said. "Somebody's been planning this since the draw was announced. Maybe longer."

He checked his watch. Forty minutes left on the clock, plus stoppage time, plus the ceremony. Call it an hour.

"I need you on the lighting rig," he said. "Find whoever's supposed to be running it tonight — the real one — and don't let them leave their post for any reason, no matter who tells them otherwise."

"And you?"

"I'm going to go have a conversation with a man and his Pikachu about timing."

---

Section 214 was loud in the way that only a World Cup final could be loud — sixty thousand voices doing the same thing at slightly different times, building toward something. Cole found his man in the second-to-last row, and sat down next to him without being invited, which is a specific kind of message.

"Good match," Cole said.

The man's Pikachu's cheeks sparked — not an attack, just nerves translating into electricity, the way some people's hands shake.

"I don't know you," the man said.

"No," Cole agreed. "But I know what's in the crate marked audio equipment, and I know which corridor it's heading for, and I know that in about thirty-eight minutes you're supposed to make this whole stadium go dark for a minute and a half."

The man didn't move. That was useful information in itself — a man with nothing to hide gets angry, or confused, or both. A man with everything to hide goes very, very still.

"There's a version of the next half hour," Cole said, "where you tell your Pikachu to sit tight, you walk out of this stadium right now, and whoever's running this with you finds out their inside man got cold feet. That version, nobody official ever hears your name. The other version involves a lot of security personnel and at least one Pokémon League inquiry, and I promise you the people who hired you will not be the ones answering those questions. They'll be on a plane."

"You don't know what you're talking about."

"I know exactly what I'm talking about," Cole said, "because I've watched a dozen people just like you decide, in the last ten seconds, that loyalty to a plan is worth less than not going to prison for it. You're not the mastermind. Masterminds don't sit in the cheap seats with their Pokémon getting jumpy. You're the guy who flips a switch. So flip a different switch. Walk."

The whistle blew below — full time, somehow already, the match having ended in the way these things do, all at once and then slowly, as the realization spreads through a crowd. Extra time. The ceremony was getting pushed back, which bought everyone more time and also meant everyone's nerves were stretched a little further.

The man's Pikachu looked up at him. Pikachu, Cole had found, were good at reading rooms — better than most people gave them credit for. This one looked at its trainer, then at Cole, then made a small, decisive sound and hopped down off the man's lap, trotting toward the exit stairs like it had made the decision for both of them.

The man watched it go for a second. Then he stood up and followed it, not looking at Cole again, which was fine. Cole hadn't needed him to look at him. He'd needed him to leave.

---

Down in the lighting control room, Mara was standing in the doorway with her arms crossed, and the actual lighting technician — a wide-eyed kid who couldn't have been more than nineteen — was looking at her like she might be the most interesting and frightening thing he'd seen all night, which, given the night, was saying something.

"Three men came by twenty minutes ago," the kid said. "Said there'd been a schedule change, said someone else would be handling the ceremony lighting cues."

"And you said?"

"I said my supervisor hadn't told me anything about that, and then this lady showed up and told me not to leave the booth no matter what, and honestly that was scarier than the three guys, so. I didn't leave the booth."

Mara's Sprigatito, perched on the console, made a satisfied sound.

Cole leaned in the doorway. "The relay team's gone. Pikachu guy walked. That leaves the crate."

"Security's already on the crate," Mara said. "I made a call."

"You made a call."

"I know people," Mara said, which was true, and which Cole had learned not to ask too many follow-up questions about.

Down on the pitch, the actual trophy — the real one, still exactly where it was supposed to be — gleamed under lights that, for the next ninety minutes at least, were staying exactly on.

"You want to stay for the ceremony?" Mara asked. "We did just save it."

Cole considered the sixty thousand people, the noise, the cameras, the inevitable questions from someone in an official-looking blazer about who exactly they were and how they'd known what they knew.

"I think," Cole said, "we should be somewhere else by the time anyone starts asking good questions."

Sprigatito, from its perch on the console, gave what could only be described as a knowing look.

They were three blocks away, in a quiet street with a taco stand and a view of the stadium lights reflecting off low clouds, when the roar went up behind them — the ceremony, the real trophy, the right hands lifting it. Cole didn't turn around. He'd already seen the part that mattered.

"Where to next?" Mara asked.

"Wherever the next 'something's wrong here' is," Cole said. "There's always another one."
